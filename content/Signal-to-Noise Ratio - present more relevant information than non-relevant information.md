> The ratio of relevant to irrelevant information in a display. The highest possible signal-to-noise ratio is desirable in design.

In each stage of [[Communication|communication]], the form of the [[Information|information]] - the signal - is degraded and extraneous information - the noise - is added. 

- Degradation reduces the amount of useful information by altering its form. 
- Noise reduces clarity by diluting useful information with useless information. 

- The clarity of information can be understood as the ratio of remaining signal to added noise.
- The goal of a good design is to produce high signal to noise ratio.


- Signal degradation occurs when information is presented inefficiently:
	- [[Language is a double edged sword which facilitates complex communication and also imposes cognitive load|unclear writing]], 
	- inappropriate graphs, or 
	- ambiguous [[Iconic Representation|icons]] and labels

- Signal clarity is improved through [[Amplification through simplification|simple and concise]] presentation of information.
- Emphasising [[Bullet-proof definition|key aspects of the information]] can also reduce signal degradation-e.g., [[Highlighting|highlighting]] or [[Redundancy|redundantly coding]] important elements in a design.

- Minimising noise means [[How to remove unnecessary details to make content more accessible?|removing unnecessary elements]], and minimising the expression of necessary elements.
- It is important to realise that every unnecessary data item, graphic, line, or symbol steals attention away from relevant elements.
- Every element in a design should be expressed to the extent necessary, but not beyond the extent necessary. [[Unnecessary exactitude|Excess]] is noise.
- Use [[Agreed standards|well-accepted standards]] and guidelines when available to leverage conventions and promote [[Consistency|consistent]] implementation.
